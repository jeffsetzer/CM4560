Lecture Notes — Contracts and Contract Administration 2

Why Contract Administration Matters

A construction contract is not a document you read once at signing and file away. It is a living rulebook that governs every notice, change, dispute, and payment for the life of the project. Poor contract administration is one of the most common — and most preventable — ways a construction company erodes its own profit margin.

🔑 Key ConceptContract administration is risk management in real time. Every notice deadline that passes unaddressed is a contractual right that may quietly disappear.

GMP Contract Anatomy — AIA A133 + A201

On the KSU Applied Technology & Construction Innovation Center project, the CM at Risk delivery method uses two linked AIA documents:

DocumentFunctionKey Sections for This Class
A133Owner–CM Agreement, establishes GMP, fee, and key datesArt. 4 (Claims/Notice incorporated by reference to A201)
A201General Conditions — governs claims, changes in the work, subcontractor obligations§5.3 (Subcontracts), §7 (Changes in the Work), §15.1 (Claims)
⚠ Teaching TipEmphasize that A201 is not the CM's contract with the owner — it's incorporated by reference. Students often assume the A133 they signed contains everything; it doesn't. The general conditions do the heavy lifting on claims and changes.

Notice of Claim — Deadlines and Consequences

A "claim" is a demand for a change in the contract sum or contract time. Most standard-form contracts require the party making a claim to give written notice within a defined window — commonly 10, 14, or 21 calendar days — from when the claimant knew or should have known of the event giving rise to the claim.

⚠ Critical Threshold — Notice WaiverMissing the contractual notice deadline typically waives the claim entirely, regardless of how legitimate the underlying cost or time impact is. The notice itself — not the final dollar figure — is what stops the clock. A CM can (and should) send notice before the cost impact is fully known, reserving the right to quantify later.

What Counts as Notice?

  1. In writing: A verbal conversation on site, however clear, typically does not satisfy a written notice requirement.
  2. Timely: Sent within the contractual window from the triggering event, not from when the cost is finalized.
  3. Specific: References the triggering event and the contract section under which the claim is made.
  4. Rights-reserving: States that cost/time impact will follow once quantified — the CM does not need a final number to give valid notice.

Change Directives vs. Change Orders

Change DirectiveChange Order
Who authorizes itOwner/Architect, unilaterallyMutual agreement — owner, architect, and CM all sign
Price & timeNot yet agreed — to be determinedAlready agreed and stated in the document
Does work proceed?Yes — CM must proceed nowYes, following execution
CM's obligationPerform the work AND preserve the right to claim cost/time via timely written noticeNone further — terms are locked in
⚡ Memory RuleA change directive is a "proceed now, argue about price later — but argue on time" instrument. Missing the notice window on a change directive is one of the most common ways CMs lose money on otherwise well-run projects.

Subcontractor Flow-Down Clauses

Flow-down clauses bind a subcontractor to the same obligations the CM owes the owner under the prime contract — including notice deadlines. If a change directive affects a subcontractor's scope, the CM must confirm the subcontract properly flows down the same notice requirement, or risk being caught in the middle: bound to the owner's deadline while unable to get timely cost information from the sub.

💡 CM NoteA well-drafted flow-down clause protects the CM by mirroring the prime contract's notice timeline in every subcontract — often with a shorter internal deadline (e.g., 5 days) so the CM has time to compile and forward notice to the owner within the prime contract's window.

How Contract Administration Failures Drive Cost

The Notice-to-Loss Chain

  1. Change Directive Issued → work must proceed under §7
  2. Notice Window Opens → contractual clock starts (commonly 10–21 days)
  3. Notice Missed or Informal → claim right waived under §15.1-style provisions
  4. Cost Impact Realized → CM has no contractual basis to recover it
  5. Margin Absorbed → cost comes directly out of company profit, not the owner's budget

A single missed notice on a mid-size change directive can cost a project tens of thousands of dollars in unrecoverable cost — money the company earned the right to claim but never asked for in writing.

📋 Talking Point
"A notice of claim doesn't have to be adversarial — it's a contractual mechanism, not an accusation." Ask students: if the CM sends notice but the cost turns out to be zero, what happens? Nothing — the notice simply expires unused. That reframes notice as low-risk, high-value discipline rather than an aggressive move.

Key Terms — Lesson 5

📋 Note
Student edition has definitions blank for note-taking. These are the complete definitions for your reference.

Guaranteed Maximum Price (GMP)

A contract sum the CM guarantees will not be exceeded, with savings typically shared with the owner per the agreement. A133

Change Directive

A written order from the owner/architect directing a change in the work before price or time impact is agreed; work must proceed. A201 §7.3

Change Order

A written instrument, signed by owner, architect, and CM, documenting an agreed change in the contract sum, contract time, or both. A201 §7.2

Notice of Claim

Written notification, delivered within the contractual deadline, that a party intends to seek a change in contract sum or time. A201 §15.1

Flow-Down Clause

Subcontract language binding the subcontractor to the same obligations (including notice deadlines) the CM owes the owner. A201 §5.3

Constructive Change

An owner action or inaction (e.g., a directive, delay, or interference) that has the effect of a change even though no formal change order was issued.

Waiver

The loss of a contractual right — such as a claim — due to failure to comply with a required condition, most often a notice deadline.

Duty to Mitigate

The obligation of a party experiencing a delay or cost impact to take reasonable steps to minimize its extent before seeking recovery.

Claim

A demand or assertion by a party seeking an adjustment of contract sum or contract time, or other relief, arising under the contract. A201 §15.1.1

In-Class Activities

Activity #1 — "Notice or No Notice?" Classification Challenge

Format: Small groups (3–4 students)
Time: 12 minutes + debrief
Ordered: Easy → Hard

Groups review each field scenario and decide: does this trigger a written notice-of-claim obligation under a standard A201-style contract? Debrief as a class — spend the most time on scenarios 5–7.

📋 Debrief Guide
Project scenarios one at a time; allow 30–45 seconds of group discussion before revealing the answer. Scenarios 1–4 are straightforward; 5–8 are genuinely trickier — scenarios 6 and 7 preview the subcontractor flow-down discussion.
Scenario 1
Owner's architect issues a formal Change Directive adding scope to the 2nd floor lab.
Notice required — §15.1/§7.3. Change directives directly trigger the CM's right (and need) to give notice preserving cost/time claims.
Scenario 2
Superintendent mentions to the architect in a hallway conversation that a recent directive "might cost a bit more."
Not sufficient notice. Verbal, informal mention does not satisfy a written notice requirement — this is exactly the trap in the scenario.
Scenario 3
A rain delay pushes the schedule back three days with no owner action involved.
Notice required if seeking time extension — most contracts require notice of weather delays within a set window (often 48–72 hours) to claim time impact, even though no cost is involved.
Scenario 4
CM discovers a minor dimensional error in the drawings that costs $200 to correct in the field.
Technically yes, practically no. The contract likely doesn't distinguish by dollar amount, but many CMs handle de minimis items informally with owner consent — a good discussion point on risk tolerance.
Scenario 5
Change Directive #4 issued 7 days ago; full cost impact from the mechanical subcontractor won't be known for two more weeks.
Notice required now, cost impact to follow. This is the exact class scenario — notice must be sent within the window regardless of whether the number is finalized.
Scenario 6
A change directive affects only the electrical subcontractor's scope; the CM's home office hasn't looped in the sub yet.
Flow-down notice required to the sub, and from the sub back to the CM, on a timeline that lets the CM still meet the owner's deadline. Preview of the flow-down discussion.
Scenario 7
CM believes the owner verbally waived the notice requirement "just this once" during a friendly site visit.
Risky — verify in writing. Verbal waivers of contractual rights are unreliable and often unenforceable; a prudent CM confirms any waiver in writing before relying on it.
Scenario 8
CM sends a notice of claim but has not yet mitigated an obvious cost driver identified in the field.
Notice is valid, but recovery may be reduced. Preview: the duty to mitigate can limit the amount ultimately recoverable even when notice was timely and proper.

Activity #2 — "The Missed Deadline" Case Discussion

Format: Pairs or individual
Time: 8 minutes + 4 min debrief

A change directive is nine days old with a ten-day notice deadline. Students identify the contract implications and the CM's next steps.

Scenario

You are Contract Administrator for the KSU Innovation Center. Thomas Brennan (project architect) issued Change Directive #4 for revised lab ventilation nine days ago. Your notice deadline is tomorrow. Mike Castellano's crew has already completed the work. No written notice has been sent yet.

  1. Has the company's right to claim cost/time on this directive been jeopardized? Why?
  2. What should happen in the next 24 hours?
  3. How would you word the notice if the final cost isn't known yet?
Answer Key:
1. Yes — the window closes tomorrow and no notice has gone out. The right is not yet waived, but it is one day from being waived entirely.

2. Send written notice today, not tomorrow. Confirm the exact date the directive was issued and the exact contractual deadline. Loop in the estimator for a rough cost range even if not final. Document everything in the project notice log.

3. Something close to: "Per A201 §15.1 and A133 Article 4, this letter serves as formal written notice of a potential claim for additional cost and/or time arising from Change Directive #4, issued [date]. A detailed cost and schedule impact analysis will follow within [X] days as required." Reserves rights without committing to an unverified number.

Ethics, Leadership & Professional Decision-Making

Notice, claims, and change directive decisions are where a construction PM's professional judgment is tested most directly — they involve real money, real deadlines, and real trust with the owner.

The PMI Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct

Responsibility

Owning the notice log. If a deadline is missed because it wasn't tracked, that's a professional failure to disclose and correct promptly — not something to quietly bury.

Respect

Sending a notice of claim does not have to damage the owner relationship. A professionally worded, well-timed notice shows respect for the contract both parties signed.

Fairness

Apply the same notice standards to your own subcontractors that the owner applies to you — don't exploit weaker sub contract language to deny a claim you'd expect the owner to honor for you.

Honesty

A notice of claim states the real basis for the claim — no inflating potential impact to create negotiating room, and no understating it to avoid an uncomfortable conversation.

🔑 SourceProject Management Institute, Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct. These four values apply directly to contract administration: a CM who inflates a claim's cost estimate to create negotiating leverage violates Honesty and Fairness even if no contract clause is technically broken.

Conflict Management in Notice & Claims

The hardest part of contract administration often isn't knowing the deadline — it's delivering the notice to an owner representative without souring the relationship. A poorly worded notice can read as adversarial even when it's simply protecting a contractual right.

⚠ The Standard Is Timely Disclosure, Not ConfrontationPMI's ethical standards call for prompt, honest communication regardless of how uncomfortable the conversation feels. Coach students toward language that is direct about the contractual requirement while remaining collaborative in tone: "Per Article 4.3, we're providing notice to preserve our rights while we work together on the actual cost impact" lands very differently than a demand letter.

Leadership in Contract Administration

A CM who tracks notices diligently and communicates them professionally builds a reputation for integrity that follows them across projects and employers. Conversely, a CM who lets deadlines slip — or who sends aggressive, poorly-timed notices — damages both the company's finances and its standing with repeat owners.

A Simple Professional Decision-Making Framework

Four Steps

  1. Identify the issue — name the deadline and the triggering event precisely, not vaguely
  2. Consider stakeholders and PMI's four values — who is affected, and which value is most at stake?
  3. Evaluate alternatives — what are the realistic options, including timing and tone of the notice?
  4. Act and document — send the notice and create a contemporaneous record of why and when

Discussion Questions

  1. Is sending a notice of claim ever "too aggressive," or is the real risk always in sending it too late?
  2. How does a CM document a notice decision so it withstands scrutiny if the claim is later disputed?
  3. What's the difference between reserving rights honestly and using notice language as a negotiating threat — and why does the line matter ethically, not just legally?
📋 Discussion Guide
Q1: Push students past "never send notice, it looks bad" — reframe: an unused notice costs nothing, a missed deadline costs real money. Q2: Look for answers mentioning dated notice logs, copies of correspondence, and internal cost-tracking memos retained per record-retention policy. Q3: The line is intent and truthfulness — reserving rights honestly states real facts and real deadlines; using notice as a threat inflates or fabricates impact to gain leverage, which violates Honesty.

Problem 1 — Notice Identification (15 pts — 3 pts each)

For each situation, state whether written notice of claim is required and cite the applicable contract concept.

  1. A change directive is issued adding 400 SF of scope to the second floor.
  2. A three-day weather delay occurs with no owner action involved.
  3. The superintendent verbally tells the architect about a cost concern during a site walk.
  4. A subcontractor's scope is affected by a change directive, but the sub hasn't been notified.
  5. The CM believes the cost impact will be under $500 and decides not to bother with notice.
Answer Key:
a. Yes — §7.3/§15.1. Change directives are a classic notice trigger; work proceeds, cost/time TBD.
b. Yes, if a time extension will be sought — most contracts require notice of delay claims within a short window regardless of cost.
c. No — verbal mention is not written notice. This is the central trap of the lesson.
d. Yes, in both directions — flow-down notice must go from CM to sub and from sub back to CM in time to meet the owner's deadline.
e. Technically yes — most contracts don't set a dollar threshold for notice, though many CMs use judgment on very minor items with owner consent.
Problem 2 — True/False with Justification (10 pts — 2 pts each)
  1. A verbal conversation with the architect satisfies a written notice-of-claim requirement.
  2. A change directive requires mutual agreement on price before work can proceed.
  3. Missing a contractual notice deadline can waive an otherwise valid claim.
  4. Flow-down clauses only protect the owner, not the CM.
  5. A CM can send notice of claim before the final cost impact is known.
Answer Key:
a. FALSE — written notice is generally required; verbal notice is not sufficient.
b. FALSE — that describes a change order. A change directive proceeds without agreed price/time.
c. TRUE — this is the central risk covered in this lesson (A201 §15.1-style provisions).
d. FALSE — flow-down clauses protect the CM by passing obligations (and matching deadlines) down to subs.
e. TRUE — notice preserves the right; the cost figure can follow once quantified.
Problem 3 — Case Study (20 pts)

You are Contract Administrator on a $28.5M GMP project. The architect issues a change directive for revised HVAC ductwork routing. Nine days pass before anyone flags it to you. Your contract's notice window is 10 days.

Part A: What is your very next action, and why does timing matter here?

Part B: Draft the notice language you would send.

Part C: What internal process failure allowed this to almost slip through, and how would you fix it going forward?

Answer Key:
Part A: Send written notice today — one day remains before the window closes. Timing matters because the clock runs from the directive's issue date, not from when someone happened to flag it internally.

Part B: Something like: "Per A201 §15.1 and A133 Article 4, this serves as formal written notice of a potential cost and/or time claim arising from Change Directive [#], issued [date]. A detailed impact analysis will follow within [X] business days."

Part C: The project lacked a live notice log tied to every change directive with automatic deadline tracking. Fix: maintain a running log (date issued, notice deadline, status) reviewed weekly, and require the person receiving any directive to log it same-day.

Contract Administration Field Checklist

📥 Career ToolPrint and use this checklist on any project involving change directives, claims, or subcontract administration. It documents your due diligence on notice and claims decisions.

✅ CM Contract Administration Checklist — Notice & Claims

Step 1: Log every change directive the day it is received — issue date, description, and the exact contractual notice deadline.
Step 2: Confirm whether the item is a change directive (proceed now, price/time TBD) or a change order (already agreed).
Step 3: Identify the specific notice-of-claim provision governing this contract (section number, not just "the contract").
Step 4: Draft and send written notice within the deadline, reserving rights even if the cost isn't finalized.
Step 5: Identify whether any subcontractor scope is affected; confirm flow-down notice is sent to and from the sub in time.
Step 6: Request a cost/schedule impact estimate from the estimator or scheduler, even a rough range, for follow-up.
Step 7: Track the notice log weekly — flag any deadline within 5 days as high priority.
Step 8: Document the final resolution (change order executed, claim withdrawn, or dispute escalated) in the project file.
